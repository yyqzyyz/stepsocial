StepSocial

A virtual practice app for people with social anxiety

CSE 400 – Human Computer Interaction

Project Overview

The Problem: Social anxiety affects 7-13% of the population. People with social anxiety want to socialize, but real-world situations exceed their coping threshold. Existing solutions (therapy, books, AI chatbots) all have gaps.

The Core Gap: No low-pressure, repeatable, non-judgmental practice environment exists.

Target Users: Young adults (18-35) with mild to moderate social anxiety.

Solution: A mobile app where users practice virtual social scenarios, receive gentle feedback, track their progress, and gradually build confidence.

Design Process

Three Design Goals

  • Goal 1: Practice a virtual social scenario – users interact with simulated conversations in a safe environment
  • Goal 2: View progress chart – users see their anxiety scores decrease over time
  • Goal 3: Watch anonymous user stories – users see others' success stories to feel less alone

User Research

I conducted research using semi-structured interviews (planned) and analyzed sources including scientific articles, Reddit posts (r/socialanxiety), and app store reviews. Six key findings emerged:

  • Users fear being judged by others
  • AI chatbots feel "fake" and unrealistic
  • Users need very low-pressure starting points
  • Users quit after one failure because of shame
  • Anonymity is critical
  • Feedback must be gentle, never critical

Key insight: Focus on emotional safety, not content quantity.

Three Core Tasks

  • T1 (Easy): Select and practice a virtual social scenario
  • T2 (Moderate): View personal progress chart
  • T3 (Complex): Unlock next difficulty level

Design Decisions

  • Calming color palette (soft blues, whites, light gray)
  • Large buttons for easy tapping
  • Short, simple text to reduce cognitive load
  • Gentle, non-judgmental feedback language
  • Progress tracking with visual indicators

Heuristic Evaluation

Another team evaluated my prototype using Nielsen's ten usability heuristics. They rated issues on a scale of 0-4.

Issues Found

  • Major Issue (3): Feedback doesn't provide helpful suggestions when users make less appropriate choices. The app says "Your response was appropriate" even when the reply wasn't ideal – users learn nothing from the experience.
  • Minor Issues (2): No visual indicator for completed scenarios; no onboarding for first-time users; no progress indicator between screens; no confirmation step before submitting.

Changes Made

  • Improved feedback to include gentle suggestions and better examples
  • Added checkmark icons for completed scenarios
  • Added a brief onboarding tutorial for first-time users
  • Added progress indicators between screens
  • Added a confirmation step before submitting answers

Reflections & Conclusion

What I learned: Heuristic evaluation catches issues designers miss. Small usability details matter for user confidence. Emotional safety is a design requirement, not a nice-to-have.

What worked well: The visual direction was solid from the start. The three-task structure kept the project focused. The evaluation feedback was actionable and specific.

What could be improved: I could have conducted user interviews earlier. More user testing would have helped validate design decisions.

How the prototype addresses user needs: The app provides a safe, low-pressure environment for practicing social interactions. Users control their pace, get gentle feedback, and can track their progress. The design prioritizes emotional safety over content quantity.
